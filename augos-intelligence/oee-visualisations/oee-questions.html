Q1 · Planned Downtime

Is your plan doing
only what's required?

Every line has planned stops — changeovers, cleaning, maintenance. These are necessary. The question isn't whether they happen. It's whether the time you allocate is honest.

We measure the gap between what was allocated and what actually happened. If your cleaning SOP says 20 minutes but the line sits idle for 45 — that overage is captured. This isn't about blaming operators. It's about asking whether the plan itself is calibrated to reality.

✦ Why This Exists · Read the Full Case Study →
Aveng Trident Steel. Three sites. 2,934 coil changes analysed. Management allocated a reasonable time for each. The data revealed individual changes ranging from 6 minutes to 90 minutes — and 537 hours of wasted time across all sites. Roodekop's standard deviation was 10,477 seconds vs PE's 840 — meaning Roodekop was 12.5× more volatile. The plan wasn't wrong because operators were slow. It was wrong because it was based on assumptions, not data. Splitting Planned Time into Allocated vs. Actual made the scheduling gap visible for the first time.
The Real Question
If two-thirds of your planned downtime is overrun, the problem isn't execution — it's assumptions. You're planning to a standard that doesn't exist. Fix the plan first. Then measure the people.
Q2 · The Human Factor

Are your people
consistent and disciplined?

This is not the same as Planning Efficiency. That's about the plan. This is about the people.

The shift runs 06:00 to 14:00 — but does it? Or does production start at 06:12 because the operator wasn't ready? End at 13:48 because cleanup started early? Do 15-minute breaks stretch to 22?

Individually — insignificant. 12 minutes here. 7 minutes there. But they compound. Across operators, shifts, weeks — into hours nobody noticed because nobody was measuring.

14h
lost to inconsistency in
executing the plan
late starts · early stops · extended breaks
✦ Why This Exists
Operators were being blamed for planning problems. "The changeover took too long" — but was the operator slow, or was the allocated time a fantasy? By creating two separate variables — Planning Efficiency (the plan) and Operator Discipline (the person) — accountability goes where it belongs. Don't blame the operator for a bad schedule. Don't blame the schedule for an undisciplined operator.
The Uncomfortable Truth
Most operations don't measure this because they don't want to know. Measuring discipline means confronting variability between people — and that's a management conversation, not a technology one. But the data makes it undeniable: same line, same task, different operator, different result. Like sport — there are first-team players and everyone else.
Q3 · Unplanned Stops

When something breaks,
can your operators handle it?

The machine stops. Unplanned. A jam, a sensor fault, a material issue. What happens next determines which side of the loss tree you fall on.

If the operator clears it themselves — no technician, no escalation — that's Operator Resolution. 40% of all unplanned downtime was resolved this way. 58 hours of problems fixed on the floor, by the people closest to the machine.

The Gold Standard
By measuring who resolves fastest for each type of stop, you find your Gold Standard operators. You don't train to an average — your best people write the rules for everyone else. OEE stops being a blame instrument and becomes a coaching framework.

The Damning Ratio
For every hour a technician spends fixing, 1.3 hours are wasted on overhead. The machine broke — unavoidable. But 56% of the response is waste. Stage parts. Pre-position tools. Document procedures. It's a logistics problem disguised as a maintenance problem.

✦ The First Breakthrough
Augos's first discovery. By splitting Performance, they found machines are quite predictable at speed. The real destroyers were invisible gaps — each lasting seconds, accumulating into hours. At one plant, cheap plastic packaging caused systematic micro-jams. The accumulated cost meant the "cheaper" option was actually the expensive one.
The Hidden Giant
True Speed Loss — 48h — is larger than all Planned downtime (42h). Nobody noticed because the line never stopped. A single OEE number is dangerous — it hides the quiet losses behind the loud ones.
