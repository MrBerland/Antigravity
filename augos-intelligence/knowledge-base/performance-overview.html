1. Capability & Mechanics

Establishing an OEE baseline is only the first step in manufacturing intelligence. True operational excellence requires proving that interventions (e.g. replacing a faulty bearing, retraining an operator, changing raw material suppliers) are actually working. The Performance Overview relies on longitudinal aggregation to prove ROI.

  • Trend Smoothing: Shifts the focus from daily noise (e.g. today's 14-minute jam) to multi-week operational trajectory.
  • Pillar Volatility: Exposes which OEE component is the most volatile. A plant that swings from 95% Availability one week to 60% the next has zero scheduling reliability, even if the monthly average looks acceptable.
  • Cross-Shift Correlation: Allows correlation of OEE trends against shift rosters or maintenance cycles.
The "Waterbed" Effect

In manufacturing, squeezing one metric often bloats another. For example, a supervisor forces the line speed up (improving Performance) but this causes significantly more jams (destroying Availability). The Performance Overview visualizes these three OEE lines over time, allowing managers to see immediately if an intervention is causing a net-positive gain or just trading one loss for another.

2. The Anatomy of a Second (The Theory of Time)

The global standard metric for manufacturing efficiency is OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness). However, from a strictly academic and practical continuous improvement standpoint, a pure percentage score (e.g., "72% OEE") is fundamentally flawed. An abstracted percentage cannot be managed, interrogated, or banked. It is a lagging indicator.

The Augos Performance Overview abandons the abstraction of percentage scores in favor of a Time-Based Decomposition (The Time Account/Time Waterfall). In this model, time is treated as a finite inventory. If a shift is scheduled for 8 hours, exactly 28,800 seconds must be mathematically verified and accounted for across rigorous definitional buckets.

The Fundamental Equation

Total Scheduled Time = Net Production Time + Σ(All Diagnosable Losses)

By forcing granularity to the absolute second, we reveal the "Invisible Losses." A manager might accept a 5% "Speed Loss" as a generic metric, but seeing it manifested as mathematically verified "12.5 Hours Lost" changes the financial calculus of the operation immediately.

4. The Human Element vs. The Machine Mechanics

What makes the Performance Overview uniquely powerful is its bifurcated data narrative. As demonstrated in the interactive component above, the interface is split cleanly into two distinct diagnostic paradigms:

The Right Panel (The Macros & Science) The Left Panel (The Human Driver & Averages)
"Where did our total time go?" "How are our people and procedures driving those losses?"
Empirical and absolute. It categorizes the physical state of the machinery into exact buckets: Net Production, Planned Loss, Quality Breakdown, Time Faulted. Translates the macro absolute into behavioral metrics categorized strictly by accountability: Operators vs. Technicians.
Example Output: 50 Hours of total time lost generically to "Operator Resolution." Example Translation: Reveals this was driven heavily because the Average Evaluation Time (time taking an operator to realize they are meant to be working on a fault) has spiked to 8 minutes per event.

This layout prevents scapegoating. A high "Total Wrench Time" on the right does not mean mechanics are inherently slow. If the left panel shows an excellent MTTR (Mean Time To Repair), the issue is not the human ability of the mechanic; rather, it is that the machine is structurally failing too often (indicated by a terrible MTBF - Mean Time Between Failures).

5. The Diagnostic Loop (Drill-Down Application)

The Performance Overview serves as the top-level aggregator. It is not designed to solve the problem directly, but to precisely identify the symptom and demand an investigation. In a high-functioning CI culture, the workflow is as follows:

  • 1. Identify the Anomaly (The "What"): Operations manager reviews the Dashboard. The right panel shows an unacceptable 14% time drain categorized directly into "Inactive Wrench Time".
  • 2. Correlate to Behavior (The "Who"): Looking left, they see the Technicians' Response Time is adequate, but the compliance logging proves delays are entirely due to waiting on the spares catalog.
  • 3. Execute the Drill-Down (The "Why"): The manager clicks the row on the dashboard. The system queries the sub-architecture (The Pareto downtime hierarchies) to list exactly which missing machine parts caused the largest portion of those waiting hours.

By migrating CI teams from subjective "efficiency reviews" into math-verified Time Accounts backed by behavioral correlations, manufacturers unlock highly targeted capital deployment and training interventions.

The API for the Performance Overview delivers an array of daily (or shiftly) snapshots, ideal for rendering time-series charts or importing into external BI tools like Tableau or PowerBI.
