Downtime Response Workflow

A rigorous examination of the granular timeline following an unplanned stop. This document outlines the exact categorical transitions between Operator Evaluation, Operator Resolution, Technician Response, and Wrench Time states.

1. The Chronology of an Unplanned Stop

When a machine faults, a precise cascade of time-state tracking begins. The Augos platform breaks away from generic "downtime" buckets by tracking the behavioral response path. Every second is attached to a specific human context.

1
Machine Faults Time Starts

An unplanned stop automatically triggers the initial time state for the work center. Because the system does not yet know if the operator will handle the fault alone or require maintenance, this initial bucket is effectively a hybrid Resolution/Evaluation Time block.

2
Operator Assessment

The operator arrives at the interface to assess the fault. Their subsequent action dictates how the previous minutes of downtime are retrospectively categorized.

2. Resolution vs. Evaluation Time

The bifurcation of Operator time is crucial for distinguishing between training gaps and genuine machine hardware failures requiring maintenance escalation.

Operator Resolution Time

If the operator clears the jam, resets the fault, and brings the machine back into operation without calling for technical assistance, the entire duration of the stop is classified as Operator Resolution Time.

Diagnosis: High average Resolution Times indicate a need for better operator training or clearer Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for clearing common jams.

Operator Evaluation Time

If the operator surveys the fault and determines they require a technician, the time leading up to that decision is locked as Evaluation Time. Once determined, the operator presses the "ASSIST" button.

Diagnosis: High Evaluation Times indicate that operators are taking too long to realize a problem is beyond their capability, hesitating to escalate, and draining valuable Net Production minutes before fixing even begins.

3. The Escalation Pivot (Tech Response Time)

The transition from Operations to Maintenance occurs at a highly specific interaction point.

3
The "ASSIST" HMI Call Eval Time Stops Response Starts

The Operator hits the ASSIST (or equivalent) button on the HMI interface. This instantly zeroes the Evaluation clock and starts the Technician Response Time clock. A digital ping/notification is routed to the maintenance team.

4
Technician Arrival (QR Scan) Response Stops Wrench Starts

The Technician physically arrives at the breakdown location. To prove their arrival and take ownership of the fault, they scan a unique Machine QR code matching the HMI error state. This action definitively ends the Tech Response Time.

Defining Tech Response Time: It is not a broad abstraction. It is the uncompromising delta between the moment the operator asked for help (Step 3) and the moment the technician physically arrived at the machine and logged in (Step 4).

4. Active vs. Inactive Wrench Time

Once the technician has scanned in, the focus shifts to the repair cycle.

Active Wrench Time

The baseline state following the QR scan. This measures the time the technician is physically at the machine, executing the repair, adjusting components, or replacing parts. It represents pure, direct maintenance effort.

Inactive Wrench Time

If the technician must leave the machine—to search for a spare part in stores, wait for a permit to work, or await electrical isolation—they pause the active clock from the HMI. This state is Inactive Wrench Time.

It represents systemic friction the mechanic faces that prevents them from executing the actual repair.

5
Production Resumes All Clocks Clear

When the repair is complete and the machine physically re-enters operational mode (detected via PLC or final HMI clear), all Wrench Time clocks are finalized. The data is pushed to the Performance Overview time account.
