01 · The Context

A coil change is one of the most frequent
planned stops in steel processing

Aveng Trident Steel operates slitting and processing lines across three sites in South Africa: Roodekop (Gauteng), Port Elizabeth (Eastern Cape), and KwaZulu Natal. Every line processes steel coils. When one coil is finished, the next one is loaded — a coil change.

Management knew this was a planned stop. They allocated time for it. The question was simple: is the time we allocate realistic? And is the process consistent across sites, products, and operators?

Quantapower (now Augos) was engaged to analyse the data — 2,934 coil change instances across all three sites, measured at millisecond resolution. Box-and-whisker plots and pivot tables were used to synthesize the data per product, per site.

Reference: Steel coil processing line — a coil change is the process of removing a finished coil and loading the next

How Opportunity Lost Is Calculated

04 · The Volatility Problem

Standard deviation tells you
what the average hides

Most operations look at averages. Averages lie. A site could average 15 minutes per coil change — but if individual changes range from 6 minutes to 90 minutes, you don't have a process. You have chaos with a polite label.

Standard deviation measures the spread. The higher it is, the more unpredictable your line becomes. PE's deviation is 12.5 times lower than Roodekop's — meaning their process is 12.5 times more predictable.

The Implication
Roodekop's standard deviation of 10,477 seconds means individual coil changes can deviate from the mean by nearly 3 hours. The varied lengths of the interquartile ranges in the box-and-whisker plots — and the extreme whisker lengths — denote great variability within a product and across different products. This points to a lack of standardization and systemization of the coil change procedure — or a lack of discipline in following it.

✦ Why PE Wins On Consistency
Port Elizabeth has the lowest standard deviation by a factor of 12.5× compared to Roodekop. Their box-and-whisker plots show tight, compact boxes with short whiskers — meaning most coil changes cluster tightly around the median. This points to a standardized procedure that is consistently followed. Roodekop's plots show wide boxes with extreme outliers — the hallmark of an undisciplined or unstandardized process.
06 · Key Inferences

The data doesn't suggest a problem.
It confirms one.

The coil change procedure is not standardized. For a given product, the process currently varies between operators, between shifts, and across sites. The wide interquartile ranges in the box-and-whisker plots — and the extreme outliers — confirm a lack of systemization.

Discipline varies dramatically by site. PE's tight distributions suggest a team that follows a consistent method. Roodekop's wide spreads suggest either no standard method exists, or it exists but isn't being followed. Both are management problems.

The question is "why so?" — why does the same procedure produce results ranging from 6 minutes to 90 minutes? The answer lies in one of three places: the method isn't defined, the method isn't trained, or the method isn't enforced. This may suggest a need to either fully automate the process or conduct a work (method & work measurement/time) study.

The Connection to OEE
This case study was conducted before the evolution of Augos's 11-variable Enhanced OEE framework. In today's language, the coil change analysis sits squarely in Planning Efficiency — measuring the gap between allocated time and actual time for planned stops. It was studies like this one that proved the need to split Planned Downtime into Allocated vs. Actual, creating one of the most powerful diagnostic variables in the system.
07 · The Path Forward

From chaos to control:
a four-step intervention

The report recommended a systematic approach — the same principle that would later drive Augos's entire product: measure, compare, standardize, sustain.

01
Benchmark to Best-Case
All products should be benchmarked against the best-case median for their respective site. This target is achievable — it already happens on the best runs. The best-case median was identified cognizant of the frequency of the product.
02
Method Study & Time Study
Conduct rigorous work measurement to document actual steps, identify waste, and build a Gold Standard coil change procedure. Iterations should lay a foundation for continuous improvement efforts.
03
Lean Thinking & 5S
Sort. Set in order. Shine. Standardize. Sustain. The 5S framework applied to the coil change work environment — tools staged, steps sequenced, waste eliminated.
04
Continuous Improvement
Iteratively reduce coil change time by measuring, intervening, and re-measuring. The coil change stoppage time should thus improve to a much lower value over time.
08 · What This Proved

This study changed how
we think about planned stops

Before this analysis, planned downtime was a single number in the OEE calculation — a lump of time accepted as "necessary." Nobody questioned whether the allocated time was realistic, or whether the actual time was consistent.

This case study proved that a single planned stop type — coil changes alone — was hiding 537 hours of recoverable production time. Not across the entire OEE tree. Not across all stop categories. Just one.

Facts from the data support that the coil change procedure is not standardized and is volatile within a product and across products. An opportunity to improve availability and maximize production time is available just within coil changes. This time, if saved, can be utilized in other productive tasks and thus enhance the OEE.
